Smart Traffic Management System

A browser-based simulation of adaptive traffic signal control using IoT sensors, edge computing, and cloud analytics — built for GIMPA Data Communications & Networking, Group 7, 2026.

Overview

This simulation demonstrates how a Smart Traffic Management System (STMS) can improve traffic flow compared to traditional fixed-timing signals. It models a 2×2 grid of 4 real Accra intersections: Tetteh Quarshie, Ako-Adjei, Circle, and Kanda.

The simulation runs two independent engines simultaneously — one using the smart adaptive algorithm, one using conventional fixed 30-second timing — fed with identical random traffic arrivals. This gives a fair, real-time comparison of both approaches.

Key finding (from SUMO simulation in the report) The smart system reduced average waiting time by 39% (from 85s to 52s), increased throughput by 32% (950 → 1,250 veh/hr), and cut emergency vehicle delay by 73% (45s → 12s).

How Traffic Signals Work

Every intersection has two signal phases that alternate: N/S green (vehicles going north or south may move) and E/W green (east/west vehicles may move). Between each green phase there is a short yellow (yield) period of 3 seconds.

Fixed Timing

Traditional traffic lights use a fixed schedule — for example, 30 seconds green for N/S, then 30 seconds green for E/W — regardless of how many vehicles are actually waiting. This is simple and predictable, but wasteful: a direction with 20 cars gets the same green time as a direction with 2 cars.

Smart (Adaptive) Timing

The smart system reads the queue length from sensors every second and adjusts the green time dynamically. A longer queue gets more green time. A short queue gets less. This means vehicles spend less time waiting at red lights overall, and the road handles more traffic per hour.

The Algorithm

The adaptive green-time formula from Section 8.3 of the project report is:

G = min(G_max, base_G + (Q ÷ sat_flow) × extra) where: G_max = 60 ← maximum green time (seconds) base_G = 20 ← minimum green time (seconds) Q = ? ← total vehicles queued on the green approaches sat_flow = 0.5 ← vehicles that clear per second per lane extra = 1.5 ← scaling factor
Worked example Suppose the North approach has 8 vehicles and South has 5 (total Q = 13).
G = min(60, 20 + (13 ÷ 0.5) × 1.5) = min(60, 20 + 39) = min(60, 59) = 59 seconds.
Fixed timing would give only 30 seconds — 29 fewer seconds of green for a heavily loaded intersection.

The algorithm is also subject to a 15-second minimum (so no phase is ever too short for vehicles to actually move) and a 60-second maximum (to prevent one direction monopolising the intersection). If both queues are empty, green time drops to 15 seconds and the cycle is very fast.

The Algorithm panel in the sidebar shows these live calculations for whichever intersection currently has the most vehicles waiting.

Emergency Vehicle Preemption

When an ambulance, fire truck, or police vehicle approaches an intersection, the system detects it via an RFID tag mounted on the vehicle (or a GPS coordinate broadcast). The system then overrides the normal signal cycle and forces a green light for the emergency vehicle's approach within 5 seconds.

In the simulation, pressing Dispatch ambulance triggers this. An ambulance appears at the front of a queue (shown as a white vehicle with a red stripe and blinking red/blue siren). After 5 ticks the signal turns green and the ambulance clears.

Comparison With a fixed-timing system, an ambulance may have to wait up to a full signal cycle (~45 seconds) before getting a green light. The smart preemption system cuts this to 5–12 seconds — a 73% reduction that can be the difference between life and death.

Scenarios

Each scenario changes the vehicle arrival rate (how often new vehicles join the queue each second) and the vehicle mix (proportion of cars, buses, and trucks, which affects how fast queues clear).

🚗
Normal

Balanced mid-day traffic across all approaches. ~25% chance of a vehicle arriving each second per lane. Mostly private cars — clears quickly. Good baseline for comparing smart vs. fixed.

🚌
Rush Hour

Heavy inbound commuter traffic. Northbound and eastbound approaches see ~50% arrival rate. More buses and minibuses in the mix, which clear more slowly than cars. Smart timing shows the largest gains here.

🌙
Late Night

Very low volume — only ~7% arrival rate. Queues stay small. Smart timing drops to near-minimum green times (15s), making cycles much shorter. Fixed timing wastes 30 seconds even when almost no vehicles are present.

⚠️
Incident

An accident blocks the North approach at Tetteh Quarshie. Vehicles divert to other approaches, causing unusual congestion patterns. Dispatch an ambulance during this scenario to see the full preemption story.

Reading the Metrics

Average Wait Time
Avg Wait · s

How many seconds, on average, each vehicle spends sitting at a red light before it can move through the intersection. Lower is better.

Throughput
TP · veh/min

How many vehicles pass through all 4 intersections combined, per minute. Higher means the roads are handling traffic more efficiently.

Active Queue
Queue · veh

Total number of vehicles currently waiting at red lights across all 4 intersections and all 16 approach lanes. A rising queue means congestion is building.

Emergency Delay
EV Delay · s

How long (in seconds) the most recent emergency vehicle waited from detection to receiving a green light. Only updated when you dispatch an ambulance.

How the comparison works Both the Smart and Fixed engines receive exactly the same random vehicle arrivals every tick. The only difference is the signal timing algorithm. This makes the comparison fair — any difference in wait time or throughput is entirely due to the algorithm, not luck.

Technology Stack

The real-world system described in the report uses the following layers. The simulation models their behaviour in software.

📡

IoT Sensor Layer

Infrared (IR) sensors detect vehicle presence. Magnetic loop detectors buried in the road count vehicles. IP cameras provide video for density estimation and emergency vehicle detection. Each intersection has 6 IR sensors, 4 magnetic loops, and 2 cameras — 12 devices per node, 48 total.

🖥️

Edge Gateway · Raspberry Pi 4

A small computer at each intersection runs the adaptive algorithm locally. It reads sensor data every 100ms, computes the optimal green time, and controls the traffic lights directly — without needing the cloud. This keeps latency under 50ms even if the internet connection drops. Aggregated data is sent to the cloud every 5 seconds.

📶

Wireless Network · 4G/LTE + LoRaWAN

4G mobile data is the primary link between edge gateways and the cloud. LoRaWAN (low-power, long-range radio) is used as a fallback for low-bandwidth status updates when 4G is unavailable. Wi-Fi handles local configuration and maintenance access.

☁️

Cloud Platform · AWS

AWS IoT Core receives sensor messages from all intersections. AWS Lambda functions process incoming data, detect anomalies, and update traffic models. Amazon DynamoDB stores all historical records for trend analysis and city planning. The cloud also hosts the monitoring dashboard that traffic officers use.

🚨

Emergency Preemption · RFID + GPS

Emergency vehicles carry RFID tags that intersection readers detect up to 300m away. As a backup, GPS coordinates broadcast from the vehicle are matched against intersection locations. When detected, a preemption signal reaches the edge gateway within 1 second and green is forced within 5 seconds.

Exporting Data

Click ↓ Export CSV in the scenario bar to download a spreadsheet of the simulation's recorded history. Data is recorded every 20 simulated seconds. The file contains:

tick, scenario, display_mode, smart_wait_s, fixed_wait_s, smart_tp_veh_min, fixed_tp_veh_min, smart_total_queue, fixed_total_queue

Open the CSV in Microsoft Excel, Google Sheets, or any spreadsheet tool to chart how wait time and throughput evolve over time, or to compare scenarios side by side.

Tip Run the simulation for at least 2 minutes at 3× speed before exporting — this gives enough data points to see meaningful trends. Switch scenarios mid-run and the export will label each row with the scenario active at that time.
